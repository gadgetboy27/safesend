There are roughly four ways professional escrow and marketplace payment systems are built:
1. What SafeSend does — PaymentIntent + Connect Transfer (Stripe-native)
This is the correct Stripe architecture for marketplaces. Stripe's own documentation calls this the "destination charge" or "separate charge and transfer" model. Braintree's marketplace product works similarly. It's used by Depop, Vinted, and most peer-to-peer marketplaces built in the last five years. The advantage is that Stripe handles PCI compliance, fraud detection, and the actual money movement. The disadvantage is you're dependent on Stripe's uptime and pricing (1.7% + 30c NZD for domestic cards eats into your 4% fee meaningfully).
2. PaymentIntent with capture_method: manual (delayed capture)
Instead of capturing funds immediately, you authorise the card and hold the authorisation. The buyer's bank ring-fences the funds but doesn't transfer them yet. When the deal completes, you call stripe.paymentIntents.capture(). This is closer to true escrow semantically — the money never leaves the buyer's bank until you release it. The catch: card authorisations expire after 7 days. For anything that might take longer than a week (slower couriers, disputes), you'd need to re-authorise, which means bothering the buyer again. This is why most escrow apps use immediate capture + refund-on-cancel instead, which is what SafeSend does.
3. Stripe Payment Links or Checkout Session (hosted page)
Rather than embedding the Stripe Element in your own frontend, Stripe hosts the entire payment page. You create a Checkout Session with payment_intent_data.transfer_group, redirect the buyer to Stripe's URL, and Stripe redirects back when done. Much simpler to implement and maintain, fully Stripe's responsibility for PCI compliance and mobile UX. Used by smaller operators who don't need a custom payment experience. The tradeoff is less control over the UI and a redirect in the flow that can feel jarring.
4. Third-party escrow services (Escrow.com, Stripe Treasury)
Escrow.com is the industry incumbent for high-value peer-to-peer transactions. They hold funds in legally registered escrow accounts, provide dispute arbitration, and are licensed in most jurisdictions. SafeSend explicitly references them in the code ("For larger transactions please use Escrow.com"). Stripe Treasury is Stripe's banking-as-a-service product that lets you create actual FDIC-insured (US) or equivalent accounts per transaction — true ring-fenced escrow. It's more complex to build and requires additional licensing in some markets, but it's what serious fintech escrow products use at scale.

The Honest Comparison
For a NZ-market peer-to-peer marketplace up to $2,500 per transaction, SafeSend's approach is the right one. Escrow.com is overkill and US-centric. Stripe Treasury isn't available in NZ yet. Delayed capture has the 7-day expiry problem. The immediate-capture + conditional-transfer model is what Depop, Vinted, and every credible marketplace built in the last five years uses.
The architecture is sound. The gaps are operational — no email notifications, no tracking polling fallback, and the auth holes from the audit. Those are all buildable. The hard financial plumbing is done correctly.
