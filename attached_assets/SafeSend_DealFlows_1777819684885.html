Side-by-Side Comparison
✅
Recommended for SafeSend
Used by Escrow.com, Depop, and most peer-to-peer marketplaces. The seller knows the item — they should define the terms. The buyer just reviews and pays. Clean, unambiguous, legally clear.
1
Seller
Creates the deal
Fills in item title, description, price, their email, and the buyer's email. Agrees to terms. Submits.
Form fields: title, description, amountNzd, sellerEmail, buyerEmail
State after: pending_buyer_payment
Advantage: seller controls the item description — no disputes about "that's not what I agreed to sell"
2
System
Emails the buyer
Buyer receives an email with the deal details and a payment link. No account needed to pay.
From: SafeSend <noreply@safesend.nz>  |  To: buyer@example.com
Subject: Alex wants to sell you a Gibson Les Paul — review and pay to secure it
Hi there,

Alex Smith has set up a SafeSend escrow deal for you:
$850.00 NZD
Gibson Les Paul 2019 Standard
Excellent condition, original case, no marks. Pick up or courier NZ-wide.

Your payment is held securely in escrow. Funds are only released to the seller once you confirm the item arrives as described.

Review Deal & Pay →
Link expires in 7 days. You'll sign in with your email — no password needed.
3
Buyer
Reviews terms and pays
Clicks the link, signs in via magic link, reviews the deal description and total, pays via Stripe. One screen.
Buyer sees: item details, SafeSend fee breakdown, total charged
Buyer agrees: terms checkbox on payment screen
State after payment: funded
4
System
Emails seller: "Payment received — ship now"
Seller is notified immediately that funds are secured and they should ship.
5
Seller
Ships and enters tracking
Ships the item, enters tracking number and courier in SafeSend. Buyer receives shipping notification.
6
Buyer
Receives item, releases funds
Confirms receipt. Funds transfer to seller. Or waits 48h for auto-release. Or raises a dispute if there's a problem.

✓ Pros

  • Seller controls item description — legally clearest
  • Buyer only has to review and pay — minimal friction
  • Both parties have clear roles from the start
  • Standard in the industry (Escrow.com, Depop)
  • Dispute resolution is simpler — seller defined the terms

✗ Cons

  • Seller needs to know buyer's email upfront
  • Seller must take the initiative — can't wait for buyer to "start" it
  • Slight friction if deal terms are still being negotiated off-platform
⚠️
Works, but creates problems
Your instinct — buyer creates, seller fills in details. Used by some payment-request tools (PayPal invoice style). Creates disputes about item description and requires a back-and-forth confirmation step that adds friction.
1
Buyer
Creates a draft deal
Enters what they think they're buying, the price they agreed on, seller's email.
Problem: buyer is describing an item they don't own. Description accuracy depends entirely on how well buyer remembers the conversation. Any mismatch becomes a dispute.
2
System
Emails seller to review and confirm
Seller must log in and verify the buyer's description is accurate before accepting.
Subject: Sam wants to buy your item — please review the deal details
Sam Jones wants to buy from you using SafeSend escrow.

They've described the deal as:
"Gibson guitar - good condition, $800"

⚠ Please review these details carefully. If anything is incorrect — price, description, or condition — you must edit before accepting.

Review & Accept →
3
Seller
Reviews, possibly edits, accepts
Seller can accept as-is or edit the description. If they edit, buyer must re-confirm. This can go back and forth.
Requires: either a fixed "seller accepts as-is" rule, or a full back-and-forth editing flow
Most implementations force seller to accept buyer's version unchanged, which sellers dislike
4
Buyer
Pays once seller confirms
Now that both parties agree, buyer pays. But this is step 4, not step 2. Extra latency, extra drop-off.

✓ Pros

  • Buyer takes initiative — good if seller is less tech-savvy
  • Buyer sees the fee before seller is involved
  • Works for "I want to buy X from you" scenarios

✗ Cons

  • Buyer describes item they don't own — accuracy issues
  • Extra round-trip before payment (seller confirm step)
  • Higher drop-off — seller may not respond to review request
  • Dispute risk: "I never agreed to those terms"
  • Requires more complex UI for edit-and-re-confirm flow
🔗
Best for negotiated deals — consider adding later
Either party creates a partial deal with a shareable link. The other party completes it. Used by PayPal.me and payment request tools. Good for when terms are still being negotiated. More complex to implement correctly.
1
Either Party
Creates a partial deal with a shareable link
Either buyer or seller fills in what they know, leaves blanks for the other party. Copies a link and sends it via WhatsApp, Facebook Marketplace chat, or email.
SafeSend already has a version of this in the current "Copy Prefilled Link" button on the new deal form — but it's implemented insecurely via URL query params that anyone can manipulate. A proper implementation uses a short-lived deal token stored server-side.
2
Other Party
Opens link, fills in their part, both confirm
The link opens a form pre-filled with what the first party entered. The second party fills in the remaining fields. Both must explicitly confirm before the deal is finalised.
3
System
Both parties sign off — deal created
Once both have confirmed the terms, buyer pays. No ambiguity about what was agreed.

✓ Pros

  • Works for either party to initiate
  • Good for deals where terms are still being negotiated
  • Both parties explicitly agree — cleanest legal position
  • Familiar from PayPal.me, Monzo payment links

✗ Cons

  • Most complex to implement correctly
  • Two-step confirmation adds latency
  • Current "prefilled link" is insecure — URL params are forgeable
  • Higher drop-off at the second-party completion step
  • Needs server-side deal token, not URL query params

Recommended approach for SafeSend right now

Ship Model 1 (Seller Creates) as the primary flow. It's the simplest to build, has the lowest dispute risk, and matches what users expect from Trade Me and Depop. Add the "Copy Prefilled Link" feature as a convenience — but implement it properly with a server-side draft token rather than URL query params. Model 3 (Joint Link) is a good v2 feature once you have real users and understand their workflow better.
