The Acceptance Flow — What Should Actually Happen
You're right that the buyer needs to approve before escrow is set up. But there's a subtle and important distinction about when Stripe gets involved:
Seller creates deal
       ↓
System emails buyer a "Review this deal" link
       ↓
Buyer opens the deal, reads the terms
       ↓                    ↓
Buyer clicks Accept    Buyer clicks Decline
       ↓                    ↓
ESCROW CREATED         Deal cancelled
Stripe PaymentIntent        Seller notified
created NOW
       ↓
Buyer enters card details
       ↓
Funds held in escrow
       ↓
Seller ships...
The key point — the Stripe escrow is created at the moment the buyer accepts and pays, not before. You don't want to create a PaymentIntent when the deal is just sitting waiting for buyer approval. That would mean creating and abandoning Stripe objects for every deal a buyer declines. The current code already does this correctly — confirm-payment creates the PaymentIntent — it just needs the acceptance gate added before that step.
So the full state machine becomes:
pending_acceptance → accepted → funded → shipped → delivered → complete
                  ↘ declined (deal dead)
The buyer clicking "Accept & Pay" does two things atomically — accepts the deal terms AND initiates the Stripe payment in the same action. One button, one screen.

The Demo Idea — Yes, But Make It Non-Functional
Keeping the form as a public-facing explainer is smart marketing. But there's an important distinction between two versions:
Option A — Fully interactive demo (no real data)
The form works exactly like the real one — fee calculator updates live, you can fill everything in, click Create Deal — but it hits a /demo endpoint that doesn't write to the database, doesn't send emails, and returns a fake deal ID. Shows a "This is a demo — sign up to create a real deal" screen at the end.
Option B — Static visual walkthrough
The form is shown in a series of screenshots or an animated preview showing the three steps. No actual interaction. Simpler to build, no risk of abuse.
Option A is better. People trust what they've touched. Letting someone fill in "Gibson Les Paul, $850" and see the fee calculator work, then see the fake deal summary, is far more convincing than a screenshot. Escrow.com does exactly this — their "how it works" page has a live fee calculator that anyone can use without an account.
The one thing to be careful about — the current form sends real emails on submission. The demo version must not do that. A /api/deals/preview endpoint that just returns the fee calculation and a simulated deal object, with no DB write and no email, is the right approach.

To answer your flow question directly: yes, you've got it right — the buyer accepts the deal and that acceptance IS the trigger for escrow setup. The two actions are combined into one button: "Review Deal & Pay". When the buyer clicks that, they're simultaneously agreeing to the terms AND initiating the Stripe payment. That's the cleanest UX — one decision, one action, money moves into escrow.
What you don't want is a two-step where the buyer clicks "Accept" (deal accepted, no money moves yet), then separately clicks "Pay" on a different screen. That creates a limbo state where the deal is accepted but unfunded, the seller thinks it's locked in, but the buyer hasn't actually committed anything. Keep it as one screen: review terms → enter card → pay → escrow created.
On the demo widget — three things to note when you embed this in your real site:
The form validation is real. It actually catches bad emails, amounts outside the $1–$2,500 range, and same buyer/seller email. The fee calculator matches the production logic exactly (4%, minimum $5). So visitors get an accurate picture of what they'd pay.
